This is something I've been wanting to address for a while. As we're exposed to more people from different backgrounds and walks of life, we inevitably run into different cultures and different understandings of reality. I've come to believe that, as a society, we need to appreciate that it's okay to live in harmony with people who see the world differently, even people whose views directly oppose ours.

The easiest example is religion, but let me start with a lighter one: flat earth versus round earth. It's okay to let someone believe what they believe. We can try to correct them, but we shouldn't burn energy being stressed about it. There's already compelling evidence that the earth is round, but we have to remember that *our* confidence in that evidence comes from *our* experiences, not necessarily theirs.

With that in mind, I've been thinking about how Christians, Catholics especially, since I'm Catholic and more exposed to that world, tend to look at people outside the faith. The instinct is often: "There's already compelling evidence that the Catholic Church is the true Church founded by Christ." But I've come to think the issue isn't the evidence. It's that the facts simply haven't been presented to everyone the same way. And even when they are presented, someone outside the faith won't receive them the way someone already inside it would.

If I talk about a saint to a Catholic, they understand immediately, because they already subscribe to the framework that makes "saint" meaningful. If I say the same thing to someone who isn't Catholic, there's an extra step required before the concept even lands, before we can get anywhere near a conversation about grace, or timing, or whether they're ready to receive what I'm presenting.

I think it's okay to accept that this person simply wasn't presented with the world the way I was. Their worldview, shaped by what they've been exposed to, is different from mine, and that makes it very hard to bring them around to my point of view.

I see the same pattern with something like homosexuality. People who have grown up immersed in that space see it as normal, some might even say they're "indoctrinated" into it. But bring the same topic to a Ugandan raised in a largely homophobic society, and it's understandable that his worldview on the matter is very different, and very hard to shift.

I recently read a tweet that stuck with me: think about how hard it is to change *yourself*, before you struggle to change another person. Your own biology, your own understanding, your own intuition all push back against you. And that's just changing yourself. Changing someone else is far harder, because now you're not just fighting your own resistance, you're up against theirs too. It's an incredibly hard force to move.

But that's not a reason to give up. What I'm really trying to say is that we should learn to live in harmony, to appreciate that our worldview isn't the only one that exists, and that other worldviews, even opposing ones, can coexist with ours. As long as we share a common goal, we can set our differences aside and work toward it together.

Think about how societies behave during a major crisis, a pandemic, a world war. In those moments, people set aside their differences, even ones as deeply felt as gender or oppression, and move forward together for the sake of survival. Once the crisis passes, the smaller disagreements resurface. But my sense is that if we can keep a shared challenge in view, not necessarily a crisis, but a bigger dream to build toward, we stay united. Elon has framed going to Mars that way: not just a mission, but a step toward becoming a multi-planetary species. A goal like that unites us and makes it easier to set our differences aside and act as one collective humanity.

That's really the core of it, and it's also why I try to be more tolerant of people with different worldviews and different religions. However much I might feel my way of seeing things is "right," I have to remember that belief is built on what I was presented with growing up, in the society I was raised in and shared with the people around me. Someone else's society presented them with something different, and their worldview might be just as valid to them as mine is to me. Even the Catholic Church, in its own teaching, acknowledges that it holds the *fullness* of salvation while recognizing that others hold a piece of it too.

So I think we can try to tolerate the people around us, especially in the moments where their views feel like they're pushing against beliefs we already hold dear.
